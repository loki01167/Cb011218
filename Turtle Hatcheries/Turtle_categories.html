GREEN TURTLE

Green turtles (Chelonia mydas) are large marine reptiles found in tropical and subtropical waters around the world. They are named for their greenish-brown carapace (upper shell) and are one of the largest species of sea turtle, reaching up to 1.5 meters in length and weighing up to 200 kilograms.

Green turtles are herbivores, feeding primarily on seagrasses and algae. They play an important role in maintaining the health of marine ecosystems by controlling the growth of seagrass beds and providing a source of food for other animals. Despite their protected status, green turtles face numerous threats including habitat loss, climate change, entanglement in fishing gear, and poaching for their meat, eggs, and shells. Conservation efforts are critical for the survival of these magnificent creatures.

OLIVE RIDLEY TURTLE

LOGGERHEAD TURTLE

The loggerhead sea turtle, has a reddish brown shell with a top shell that resembles a little heart and a sizeable head. A typical adult is three feet long and weighs around 113 kilograms (250 pounds).

The females of these marine reptiles briefly come ashore to lay their eggs in bays, lagoons, and salt marshes, although they are mostly found in saline water, often hundreds of kilometres out at sea. Being carnivores, they mostly eat marine invertebrates, particularly crabs with hard shells, which they eat with their mighty jaws. The average lifespan of a loggerhead sea turtle is estimated to be 50 years.

HAWKSBILL TURTLE

These sea turtles range in size from tiny to larger, and they have a hawk-like curved, pointed beak. Their bright and erratic shell design allows them to be easily differentiated from their cousins. Due to their extensive migratory patterns, hawksbills may be found in tropical and subtropical areas of the Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans.

These turtles are omnivores, eating anything from algae to sea urchins. However, favored foods are sponges, which are poisonous or disagreeable to other marine life. Despite having a protective shell, this species is hunted by huge fish, sharks, crocodiles, and octopuses. The young turtles are susceptible to beach crabs, coast dogs, and birds, much like other turtle hatchlings.

LEATHERBACK TURTLE

They may reach heights much over 160 cm and weigh over 700 kg. This species' shell resembles leather in texture and is softer and more flexible than the green turtle's. These marine critters can live in colder temperatures and dive to depths of up to 1000 m because of their thick covering of insulating fat.

These enormous creatures are particularly vulnerable to floating plastic waste in our waters since jellyfish are their preferred meal. This turtle species is considered vulnerable by experts. Rarely, leatherback turtles have been spotted off the coast of Sri Lanka around Kosgoda, Mawolla, or Rekawa beach.
